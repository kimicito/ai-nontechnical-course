10.2 Tasks Inside GENAi: On-Prem

20 min · for PR department · closed loop

The PR department works with information that is rarely public: crisis scenarios, internal communications, positions for regulators, stakeholder data, draft statements before publication. Any leak of this can cause reputational or legal damage.

Solution: on-prem LLM (e.g., Qwen, GigaChat Enterprise) within the company's closed perimeter. Data never leaves the perimeter, but the LLM accelerates material preparation by 5–20x.

Why On-Prem for the PR Department

What cannot be sent to the cloud:
  • Crisis scenarios and plans before publication
  • Draft statements at the moment of an incident
  • Stakeholder, regulator, and interaction history data
  • Internal management emails about layoffs, restructuring, sanctions
  • Reputational risk assessments by region and segment
  • Board reports with sensitive metrics
What can be done on-prem:
  • Generate crisis scenarios "if X happens"
  • Prepare a draft statement minutes before publication
  • Conduct a post-mortem analysis of an incident using internal data
  • Write an internal management email in the right tone
  • Compile an FAQ for employees on sensitive topics
  • Prepare a position for a regulator based on internal documents

1. Crisis Plan Preparation

A PR specialist prepares "what if" scenarios. Previously, this took days of work by scriptwriters and lawyers. With an on-prem LLM — systematic scenario generation, checking for missed risks, and structuring the response plan.

Prompt Example
Our company operates in industry [X] in [regions].

Prepare a crisis plan for 10 scenarios:
1. Customer data leak
2. Production accident with casualties
3. Accusation of top-manager corruption
4. Sanctions/ban on operations in a key region
5. Mass layoffs (leaked to media before official announcement)
6. Product boycott for ethical reasons
7. Cyberattack with operational shutdown
8. Product recall for safety reasons
9. Public conflict with a key partner
10. Fake news about bankruptcy

For each scenario:
- Scenario development (best and worst case)
- Who is responsible (roles)
- Response timelines (first minute, first hour, first day)
- Key messages (hold statements)
- Communication channels (internal and external)
Input format: business description, regions, industry, risk register file (optional).
Output format: structured crisis plan with scenario tables and hold statements.
Time savings: 3–5 days → 30 minutes for a draft.
Why ONLY inside: a crisis plan is an operational instruction on how the company responds to threats. A leak gives competitors and malicious actors a roadmap of weak points.

2. Statement Preparation in the Moment of Crisis

In the first minutes of a crisis, speed is critical. A statement is needed that acknowledges the fact, does not prematurely confirm guilt, does not escalate, and preserves reputation. On-prem LLM prepares a draft in seconds based on facts.

Prompt Example
An incident has occurred: [brief description].

Known facts:
- Date and time
- What happened (no speculation)
- Scale (if known)
- What has already been done
- What is still unknown

Prepare 3 versions of the official statement:
1. Restrained — for initial publication (website, social media)
2. Detailed — for the press, if inquiries are already coming in
3. Internal — for employees, before official release

Requirements:
- Acknowledge the fact, do not confirm guilt prematurely
- Indicate that an investigation is underway
- Provide a media contact
- No emotions, no excuses, no legally risky wording
Input format: incident facts (short brief, text, or voice transcript).
Output format: 3 ready statement drafts + channel and timing recommendations.
Time savings: 1–2 hours → 5 minutes.
Why ONLY inside: any statement before publication is an unapproved position. A leak of a draft creates a false narrative and can become evidence in court.

3. Post-Mortem Analysis After an Incident

After a crisis, it's important to understand: what worked, what didn't, what communication mistakes were made, what lessons were learned. Internal assessment is not for the public — it's for improving processes.

Prompt Example
The incident is concluded. Here is the internal data:
- Chronology (file Chronology.xlsx)
- All external communications (file Communications.pdf)
- Media coverage (file Monitoring.docx)
- Internal correspondence (anonymized)

Conduct a post-mortem analysis:
1. Response timeline: when was what done, what was delayed
2. Did the hold statements work? Which ones had to be adjusted?
3. Which channels were effective, which were not
4. Communication mistakes (wrong tone, inaccuracies, omissions)
5. Compliance of actual actions with the plan (if there was one)
6. Recommendations: what to change in the crisis plan

Format: internal document, no external quoting.
Input format: chronology, communications, media monitoring, internal correspondence (within the perimeter).
Output format: structured post-mortem with timeline and recommendations.
Time savings: 1 day → 15 minutes.
Why ONLY inside: internal correspondence and admissions of mistakes are material for lawsuits and regulatory inspections. Any leak to the cloud is a potential find for the opposing side.

4. Internal Emails and Management Letters

A CEO writes a letter to employees about restructuring, sanctions, or strategy changes. Tone, structure, legal wording — everything is critical. The LLM helps with a draft that lawyers and HR then review.

Prompt Example
The head of division [X] is writing a letter to employees of division [Y] (N people).

Topic: [layoffs / strategy change / exit from region / sanction restrictions]

Key messages:
- [fact 1]
- [fact 2]
- [what this means for employees]
- [next steps]

Tone: honest, no panic, respectful, no corporate jargon.

Prepare 3 versions of the letter:
1. Soft — for teams with high engagement
2. Business-like — for functional departments
3. Brief — for wide distribution (key facts, link to details)

Add: a checklist of what needs to be approved by HR and legal before sending.
Input format: topic, key messages, audience (number, department type).
Output format: 3 letter versions + approval checklist.
Time savings: 2–3 hours → 10 minutes.
Why ONLY inside: internal emails about layoffs, restructuring, or sanctions are insider information. A leak before the official announcement creates panic, distorts the market, and can lead to legal consequences.

5. FAQ for Employees on Sensitive Topics

After major changes, employees ask the same questions. HR and PR prepare an FAQ, but it takes time. The LLM accelerates preparation, and HR and legal then validate it.

Prompt Example
Company [name] is conducting [restructuring / layoffs / exit from region / responding to sanctions].

Compile an FAQ for employees with 15 questions:
- 5 questions about personal consequences (job, compensation, relocation)
- 5 questions about the process (timelines, criteria, who makes decisions)
- 5 questions about the company's future (strategy, new markets, support)

Requirements:
- Answers are honest but not panic-inducing
- If the answer is not yet known — honestly say the process is in progress
- No promises the company cannot guarantee
- Links to internal resources (HR, hotline, portal)

Format: question — brief answer — who to contact for details.
Input format: description of change, list of known facts, internal regulations (optional).
Output format: FAQ of 15 questions with short answers and directions.
Time savings: 4–6 hours → 10 minutes.
Why ONLY inside: the FAQ contains details of restructuring, compensation, layoff timelines — this is confidential HR information. Getting into the cloud = risk of leak before the official announcement.

6. Position Preparation for Regulator Interaction

A regulator requests a position on an issue. The response must be accurate, legally correct, but protect the company's interests. Position preparation involves working with internal documents and analyzing regulatory practice.

Prompt Example
Regulator [name] has requested a position on the issue: [essence of request].

Input data:
- Internal documents on the topic (files)
- Previous company responses to similar requests
- Regulatory practice (internal database)
- Legal memorandum (file)

Prepare:
1. Brief position (1 page) — main thesis, 3 arguments, request to the regulator
2. Detailed position (5 pages) — with quotes from documents, references to practice
3. Risk checklist: what cannot be said, which wording is dangerous

Tone: constructive, respectful, but firm in defending the company's position.
Input format: regulator request text, internal documents, legal memoranda, previous responses.
Output format: brief and detailed position + risk checklist.
Time savings: 1–2 days → 30 minutes.
Why ONLY inside: a position for a regulator is built on internal documents, legal assessments, and strategic priorities. This can be used in judicial or regulatory proceedings. A leak undermines the negotiating position.

7. Stakeholder Briefings

Before an important meeting or investor meeting, a quick profile is needed: who this is, interaction history, sensitive topics, previous public statements. On-prem LLM analyzes internal CRM and communication archives.

Prompt Example
Prepare a briefing on the stakeholder: [name / organization].

Input data:
- Interaction history (file CRM_history.xlsx)
- Previous meetings and correspondence (files)
- Public statements (internal monitoring database)
- Known preferences, risks, conflicts of interest

The briefing should include:
1. Profile (role, influence, interests)
2. Interaction history (timeline of key events)
3. Sensitive topics (what cannot be touched)
4. Potential allies and opponents
5. Approach recommendations (tone, arguments, meeting format)
6. Red flags (risks to minimize)
Input format: CRM data, correspondence, internal meeting notes, monitoring database.
Output format: stakeholder profile with timeline, risks, and recommendations.
Time savings: 2–3 hours → 10 minutes.
Why ONLY inside: stakeholder briefings contain personality assessments, conflicts of interest, internal negotiation tactics. This is the company's strategic intelligence, which in the cloud becomes compromising material.

8. Reputational Risk Analysis by Region of Presence

The company operates in 10+ countries. In each region — its own risks: political, regulatory, cultural, competitive. The LLM analyzes internal regional data and produces a risk map.

Prompt Example
The company has operations in regions: [list].

Input data:
- Internal regional reports (files)
- Incident and claim history (file)
- Regional media monitoring (file)
- Regulatory inspection data (file)

Conduct a reputational risk analysis for each region:
1. Political risk (power changes, sanctions, protectionism)
2. Regulatory risk (new laws, inspections, fines)
3. Cultural risk (brand, product, communication — what is wrong)
4. Competitive risk (attacks from competitors, price wars)
5. Media risk (topics that have already surfaced, media tone)

For each region:
- Risk assessment (high / medium / low)
- Key indicators (what to watch)
- Mitigation recommendations
- Escalation scenario (what to do if the risk materializes)
Input format: internal regional reports, incident history, media monitoring, regulatory inspection data.
Output format: risk map by region with assessments, indicators, and scenarios.
Time savings: 1 week → 30 minutes.
Why ONLY inside: regional risk assessments are based on internal data about problems, incidents, and regulatory inspections. This is information that can affect investment decisions and negotiations with partners. A leak is a blow to the commercial position.

9. Board Reports on Reputational Metrics

Once a quarter, PR prepares a report for the board of directors: tone, reach, key topics, dynamics. Previously, this took days of analyst work. With on-prem LLM — automatic generation from internal monitoring data.

Prompt Example
Based on monitoring data for the quarter (file Monitoring_Q3.xlsx), prepare a report for the board of directors:

1. Executive summary (1 page): main changes, 3 theses
2. Reputational metrics:
   - Reach (by channels)
   - Tone (positive / neutral / negative)
   - Share of voice vs competitors
   - Top 10 topics
3. Key incidents: what happened, how we responded, result
4. Dynamics vs previous quarter (trends)
5. Risks on the horizon (what may flare up in Q4)
6. Recommendations (3 points on what to do)

Tone: professional, data-driven, no fluff. Every thesis is backed by numbers or an example.
Input format: internal monitoring (Excel, CSV), reach data, tone, topics.
Output format: structured board report with summary, metrics, risks, and recommendations.
Time savings: 2–3 days → 20 minutes.
Why ONLY inside: a board report contains risk assessments, incident mentions, and strategic recommendations. This is insider information for investors and regulators. A leak = market manipulation or reputational damage.

10. Speaker Preparation for Interviews on Sensitive Topics

A speaker goes for an interview on a sensitive topic. Q&A needs to be prepared, vulnerable points identified, and wording practiced. On-prem LLM models aggressive questions and prepares answers that won't harm the company.

Prompt Example
Speaker: [name, position].
Interview: [media name, format — live / recorded / debate].
Topic: [sensitive topic].

Known vulnerable points:
- [fact 1 that can be used against us]
- [fact 2 that requires delicate wording]
- [topic we want to avoid]

Prepare:
1. 20 questions that may be asked (including 5 "digs" — aggressive, provocative)
2. Recommended answers for each question (2 versions: brief and detailed)
3. Trap phrases: what NOT to say (with explanation why)
4. Tactic to seize initiative: how to bring the conversation back to key messages
5. Preparation checklist (what to have on hand, who can be mentioned, what to avoid)
6. Post-interview: what to pay attention to in the recording before publication
Input format: speaker profile, interview topic, list of known vulnerable points, internal data (optional).
Output format: Q&A with 20 questions, answers, traps, tactics, and checklist.
Time savings: 4–6 hours → 15 minutes.
Why ONLY inside: interview preparation contains explicit admissions of vulnerable points, phrases to avoid, and takeover tactics. A leak of this material is a gift to a journalist: he knows where to hit and what the company is afraid to say.
